01

Nucleus

Six protons fix its identity as carbon; six neutrons in the most common isotope, carbon-12, bring the mass number to twelve.

02

Electron shells

Two electrons fill the inner K shell. The remaining four sit in the outer L shell — never full, always ready to bond.

03

Valence

Four outer electrons let carbon form up to four covalent bonds at once, the reason it can build chains, rings, and lattices.

04

Isotopes

Carbon-14, with two extra neutrons, decays slowly and predictably — the basis for radiocarbon dating of ancient remains.

One element, several architectures

Diamond

Every atom locked to four neighbours in a rigid 3D lattice — the hardest natural material.

Graphite

Flat sheets of hexagons stacked loosely — layers slide past each other, which is why pencils write.

Fullerene

Sixty or more atoms curved into a hollow cage, like the panels of a soccer ball.

Amorphous

No long-range order at all — soot and charcoal, carbon atoms bonded in irregular clusters.
